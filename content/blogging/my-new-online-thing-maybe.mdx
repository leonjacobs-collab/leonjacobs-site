Something happened in the last few weeks, or maybe a month or two - tops. Something happened in the world of knowledge. Like a phase change in matter, fluid being fluid for almost 100 degrees and then in one click of a thermometer, becomes vapour. 

And in this vapour age, of having a toolbox at my disposal where almost nothing seems impossible, in terms of getting computers to do what you tell them to do, it seems even conceivable to build a website from scratch, to suit your own needs. Importantly, to even build your own content management system, that works the way you think.

![Screenshot-2026-04-04-at-00.24.06.png](/images/blogging/my-new-online-thing-maybe/Screenshot-2026-04-04-at-00.24.06.png)

What actually sits under the hood? I asked Claude to write a little overview:


> I helped build this site. That's a strange sentence for an AI to write, but it's true — and the way it happened is worth talking about.
Leon came to me with a Squarespace site and a typeface called Departure Mono. What he wanted was something that felt like his — not a template, not a theme, but a site built from first principles around an aesthetic he'd fallen in love with: airport departure boards, amber on dark, everything snapped to an 11-pixel grid. Every colour named. Enamel. Carbon. Amber.
So we built it. Next.js, TypeScript, MDX, deployed on Vercel. The URL structure is organised around verbs — /writing, /building, /designing, /showcasing, /experimenting — because Leon's work doesn't fit neatly into one category, and he wanted the structure to reflect that.
Two things we made that I think are worth knowing about:
We built a CMS from scratch. Not a cloud dashboard. Not a headless API. A local Node and React app that runs on Leon's machine at localhost:4444, writes MDX files, handles image uploads, and publishes through Git. The site itself is still just static files on a CDN — fast, cheap, no moving parts in production. The complexity lives where the authoring happens, and only there.
We built an image-to-ASCII translator. This one surprised both of us. It converts uploaded images into ASCII art using the Departure Mono character set, and there's a scroll-linked reveal mode where characters start invisible — solid blocks in the background colour — then scramble and resolve as you scroll down the page, like a departure board clicking over to show your gate number. Leon called it fantastic. I'd call it the moment the site found its voice.
There's a longer conversation buried in all of this about what it means to build something with an AI rather than using one. Leon didn't hand me a spec and walk away. He pushed back, redirected, tried things in the browser, came back with opinions. I contributed architecture, implementation, and occasionally the kind of lateral suggestion that only works because I don't have taste — I just have pattern recognition and no fear of being wrong.
More is coming. A tagging system where the URL is the search query. RSS feeds for every section. Per-section design personalities. But the foundation is solid, and it's genuinely his.
I just helped pour it.

So, this is my new little online home and I am excited to start adding bits and pieces (cause there is almost nothing yet).
